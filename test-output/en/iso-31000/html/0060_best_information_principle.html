Purpose

This document describes the "Best available information" principle of ISO 31000 risk management. Inputs to risk management are based on historical and current information as well as future expectations.

Principle Description

Inputs to risk management are based on historical and current information as well as future expectations. Risk management explicitly takes into account any limitations and uncertainties associated with such information and expectations.

Key Statements

  • Decisions based on best available data
  • Historical data is analyzed
  • Current information is considered
  • Future developments are anticipated
  • Uncertainties are explicitly addressed

Implementation in AdminSend GmbH

Information Sources

Information Type Sources
Historical data Risk register, incident database, audit reports
Current information Monitoring systems, KPIs, reports
Future expectations Market analyses, trend studies, expert opinions
External data Industry reports, regulatory updates

Data Quality

Quality Criteria: - Accuracy: Correctness of information - Completeness: Scope of available data - Timeliness: Currency of information - Relevance: Significance for decisions - Reliability: Trustworthiness of sources

Dealing with Uncertainties

Methods for Uncertainty Treatment: 1. Sensitivity analyses 2. Scenario planning 3. Monte Carlo simulations 4. Expert estimates with confidence intervals 5. Documentation of assumptions and limitations

Information Management

Data Collection and Analysis

  • Systematic capture of relevant information
  • Regular update of data basis
  • Analysis of trends and patterns
  • Validation of information
  • Documentation of data sources

Knowledge Management

  • Lessons learned database
  • Best practice collection
  • Document expert knowledge
  • Promote knowledge exchange
  • Continuous knowledge update

Decision Making

Information-Based Risk Decisions

Decisions in risk management consider: - Available data and its quality - Known uncertainties and limitations - Expert opinions and experiences - Cost-benefit ratio of information gathering - Time urgency of decision
